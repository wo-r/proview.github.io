Disinhibited Social Engagement Disorder and Reactive Attachment Disorder: A Comprehensive Overview

Understanding the impact of early childhood trauma and caregiving disruptions on mental health is crucial for recognizing disorders like Disinhibited Social Engagement Disorder and Reactive Attachment Disorder. Both are attachment disorders typically affecting children who have experienced neglect, abuse, or severe disruptions in caregiving during their formative years. While these disorders share some common causes and symptoms, they manifest in distinct behavioral patterns. In this article, we will delve into the definitions, symptoms, causes, and treatments for both disorders, comparing their similarities and differences, and exploring how they might relate to the real-life case of David Berkowitz, the infamous "Son of Sam."

What is Disinhibited Social Engagement Disorder?

Disinhibited Social Engagement Disorder (DSED) is a relatively rare and newly defined attachment disorder. It was officially introduced in the DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th edition) in 2013. DSED primarily affects children who have undergone significant neglect, abuse, or disruptions in caregiving early in life. Children with DSED may approach and interact with unfamiliar adults without fear, hesitation, or regard for social boundaries.

What are the symptoms?

Children with DSED often show excessive sociability towards unfamiliar adults, greeting them with no caution or appropriate social distance.
These children may engage in indiscriminate behavior such as hugging, climbing onto strangers' laps, or initiating physical contact without any social cues.
One of the most concerning symptoms is a failure to form secure, selective attachments to primary caregivers. Instead, the child may seek attention and affection from anyone whether they are familiar or strangers, often at the expense of deeper emotional bonds.
Children may lack empathy or understanding of social cues. They may not recognize when their behavior is inappropriate, leading to significant interpersonal difficulties.

What are the symptoms?

Children with inhibited type may appear emotionally distant or avoidant, refusing to seek comfort from caregivers or respond when comfort is offered.
Children with inhibited type often exhibit numbness or a lack of typical emotions like joy, sadness, or anger.
Similar to DSED, children with disinhibited type may engage in indiscriminate social behavior, such as seeking affection from strangers inappropriately.
Children with disinhibited type may have a superficial attachment to caregivers, showing little emotional depth in their relationships.
Children with inhibited type may struggle to form any meaningful, trusting relationships with caregivers or peers.

What is Reactive Attachment Disorder?

Like DSED, Reactive Attachment Disorder (RAD) also stems from early childhood trauma or caregiving disruptions. However, RAD is generally considered a more severe condition. Children with RAD have significant difficulties forming emotional bonds with their caregivers, which often leads to withdrawal or emotional numbness. RAD is divided into two subtypes: Inhibited Type and Disinhibited Type, each presenting differently.

Similarities and Differences Between DSED and RAD

Although DSED and RAD both arise from early childhood trauma, they manifest differently in terms of behavior

Similarities

Both disorders are characterized by significant attachment difficulties, making it hard for children to form healthy emotional bonds.
Both DSED and RAD are strongly associated with early childhood neglect, abuse, or disruptions in caregiving.
Treatment for both disorders focuses on attachment-based therapy and caregiver support to help the child form secure emotional bonds.
Both disorders benefit from a stable and predictable caregiving environment, which helps children feel safe and begin to heal.

Differences

DSED children are overly familiar with strangers, exhibiting inappropriate behaviors like hugging or climbing into strangers' laps.
RAD children, particularly in the Inhibited Type, are often withdrawn, avoiding social interaction and not seeking comfort from their caregivers.
While DSED children may struggle with attachment to both caregivers and strangers, RAD children typically have a deeper emotional withdrawal, struggling particularly to attach to their primary caregivers.
